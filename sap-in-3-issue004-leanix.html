Last week in Issue 003, SAP Signavio answered the first question every transformation must ask: do we actually understand how our business processes work today? Process mining replaced assumption with evidence, giving organizations a data-accurate picture of their current-state operations before redesigning them. That foundation is essential. But it surfaces a second question — one that is equally important and equally underestimated. Now that we understand our processes, do we have the right technology landscape to support where we need to go?

For most large organizations, that question is harder to answer than it should be. Enterprise technology landscapes have grown organically over decades — layer upon layer of applications, integrations, and platforms accumulated through acquisitions, departmental decisions, and technology cycles that nobody fully mapped along the way. SAP LeanIX is the platform built to bring clarity to that complexity. As Part 2 of our Business Transformation Trilogy, this issue explains why enterprise architecture intelligence is the missing link in most transformation programs, how LeanIX delivers it, and what changes when technology leaders can finally see the full picture of what they own, what it costs, and what needs to change.

Finding One — Why
01
The Hidden Cost of Not Knowing What Technology You Actually Have

There is a number sitting inside most enterprise IT organizations that nobody talks about openly — the true count of applications running across the business. Ask the CIO and they will give you an estimate. Ask the CFO and they will give you a different one. Ask the business units and the numbers diverge further still. The reality, when organizations actually conduct a comprehensive technology inventory, is almost always larger than any individual estimate — and the gap between what leadership believes they have and what is actually running is one of the most consistent sources of wasted technology spend in the enterprise.

Research consistently shows that large enterprises run between 900 and 1,200 applications on average — with significant duplication, shadow IT, and end-of-life systems that no longer align to current business strategy. The annual cost of maintaining that complexity — in licensing, integration overhead, security exposure, and the organizational friction of operating incompatible systems — runs into the tens of millions for organizations of meaningful scale. More critically, this architectural opacity makes transformation planning extraordinarily difficult. When you don't have an accurate, current picture of what technology supports which business capabilities, every decision about what to change, replace, or retire is made against incomplete information. SAP LeanIX was built to eliminate that blind spot permanently.

The Enterprise Architecture Blind Spot · Three Dimensions of Technology Opacity
🔴
Application Sprawl — The Invisible Tax
The average large enterprise runs far more applications than any single leader can account for. Duplicate tools performing the same function, legacy systems nobody has decommissioned, departmental SaaS subscriptions that bypassed IT procurement — all accumulate into a technology landscape that consumes budget, creates security exposure, and resists standardization. Without a single source of truth for the application portfolio, rationalization efforts are slow, incomplete, and frequently restart from scratch after leadership changes.
🟡
Transformation Planning Without a Map
When a CIO launches a cloud migration, an S/4HANA implementation, or a post-merger integration without an accurate enterprise architecture map, the discovery phase of that program becomes its most expensive phase. Dependencies nobody knew existed surface mid-implementation. Applications assumed to be retired turn out to be actively used. Integration points undocumented in any system diagram create cascading delays. The cost of discovering architectural complexity during transformation is always dramatically higher than the cost of mapping it beforehand.
✅
The Strategic Opportunity — Architecture as a Decision Tool
Organizations with a live, continuously maintained enterprise architecture map — showing every application, its business capabilities, its technology health, its integration dependencies, and its cost — make technology investment decisions from a position of genuine clarity. They can identify redundancy, model the impact of decommissioning a system, prioritize migration sequences based on dependency chains, and present boards with data-driven technology roadmaps. That capability is the difference between IT as a cost center and IT as a strategic partner.

Finding Two — How
02
How SAP LeanIX Builds and Maintains Your Enterprise Architecture Intelligence

SAP LeanIX is an enterprise architecture management platform that creates and continuously maintains a single source of truth for an organization's entire technology landscape. Unlike traditional enterprise architecture tools — which produce static diagrams that become outdated the moment they're published — LeanIX is built around a live data model that integrates with the systems it documents, pulling updates automatically and surfacing changes in real time. The result is an architecture map that stays current without requiring manual maintenance effort, and that serves as an active decision-support tool rather than a historical record.

The platform organizes technology intelligence around business capabilities — connecting each application to the business function it supports, the processes it enables, and the strategic outcomes it contributes to. That capability-based model is what makes LeanIX transformationally useful rather than just informationally useful. It allows a CIO to answer not just "what applications do we have?" but "which applications support our most strategically critical capabilities?" and "what is the technology health and obsolescence risk of the systems our most important processes depend on?" Those questions — answered with data rather than intuition — form the foundation of credible, defensible technology investment decisions.

Finding Three — What
03
What Changes When Architecture Becomes a Strategic Asset, Not an IT Artifact

The business outcomes from SAP LeanIX arrive across three dimensions that collectively redefine the role of enterprise architecture in organizational decision-making. The first is cost transparency. Organizations that deploy LeanIX consistently discover application redundancy they didn't know existed — duplicate tools performing the same function across business units, shadow IT subscriptions that bypassed procurement, legacy systems consuming licenses for capabilities that have been replaced. The rationalization opportunity identified in the first year of LeanIX deployment typically represents 20–30% of the application portfolio — and the cost savings from eliminating that redundancy frequently deliver a compelling standalone ROI.

The second dimension is transformation confidence. When a technology roadmap is built on LeanIX data — grounded in an accurate, current picture of what exists, what it costs, and how it connects — the quality of that roadmap is fundamentally different from one built on stakeholder interviews and outdated documentation. Implementation teams encounter fewer surprises. Dependency conflicts surface in planning rather than execution. Migration sequences are optimized against real architectural constraints rather than assumed ones. The third dimension is strategic credibility — the ability of technology leaders to present boards and executive committees with data-driven investment cases that connect architecture decisions to business outcomes in language that finance and operations can evaluate and approve. That credibility, built on LeanIX's analytical foundation, transforms the CIO from a cost center manager into a strategic transformation partner.
